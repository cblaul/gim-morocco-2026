Airport Transfer: Charles de Gaulle → Orly
Critical • Allow 3–4 hours

Why this matters: You land at Charles de Gaulle Terminal 1 (United) at 6:55 AM and depart from Orly Terminal 4 (Royal Air Maroc) at 12:10 PM. These are separate tickets on separate airlines — your bags will NOT auto-transfer. You must collect your checked luggage at Charles de Gaulle and re-check at Orly.

Recommended Route — RER B + Metro Line 14
~70 min total • ~€14 • Fewest transfers, easiest with luggage
1
Land at Charles de Gaulle Terminal 1 (6:55 AM) — United flies into Terminal 1. Follow signs for "Baggage Claim / Sortie" (Exit). Go through passport control (EU entry) and collect your checked bags from the carousel.
2
Follow signs to RER B trains. From Terminal 1, take the free CDGVAL shuttle to the "Aéroport Charles de Gaulle 1" RER B station. Look for signs saying "RER B" or "Trains."
3
Buy a "Paris Region ↔ Airports" ticket (~€14) at the ticket machines. This single ticket covers both the RER B and Metro Line 14 all the way to Orly. You can also buy on your phone via the Île-de-France Mobilités app.
4
Take RER B southbound to Châtelet–Les Halles (~35 min). Any RER B train heading toward Paris will stop here — it's a major hub. You can't miss it.
5
Transfer to Metro Line 14 at Châtelet–Les Halles. Follow signs for Line 14 → Aéroport d'Orly. This is a modern, automated metro — it's fast and runs frequently. Orly is the last stop (~30 min).
6
Arrive at Orly, walk to Terminal 4. Metro 14 drops you at Terminal 1-2-3. Follow signs to Terminal 4 (short walk or free shuttle). Head to Royal Air Maroc check-in counters, drop your bags, and go through security. Aim to arrive by ~10:00 AM for a 12:10 PM departure.

Alternative: Taxi or Uber
If you'd rather skip public transit with luggage, a taxi from Charles de Gaulle → Orly runs 40–70 min and costs ~€57–95 depending on traffic. Follow signs for "Taxis" at arrivals. Faster, zero hassle — split with classmates to save.

Culture Prep
Key cultural knowledge for Morocco — from the GIM Travel Guide. Read this before you go and reference it on the plane.

Morocco at a Glance: Constitutional monarchy led by King Mohammed VI. GDP growing 3.7–4.9%, driven by automotive, aerospace, and renewable energy. Hosting the 2030 FIFA World Cup. A rapidly modernizing country with deep cultural traditions.

Language
What You'll Hear
Moroccan Arabic (Darija) is the most widely spoken language. French is the primary business language and widely spoken in cities. Standard Arabic and Amazigh (Berber) are also official.

Key Phrases:
Salaam Alaikum — Hello (Peace be upon you)
Shukran — Thank you
La — No  /  Na'am — Yes
Bslama — Goodbye
Bezzaf — Too expensive (useful in souks!)
Currency & Money
Moroccan Dirham (MAD)
1 USD ≈ 9.28 MAD (as of March 2026)

Morocco is a cash-based society. Many shops, taxis, and markets accept cash only. ATMs are widely available — withdraw MAD on arrival at the airport.

Credit cards are accepted at hotels, upscale restaurants, and modern stores. Visa and Mastercard work best. Tip 10–15% at restaurants. Small tips (5–10 MAD) expected for services.
Dress Code & Etiquette
How to Show Respect
◆ Dress modestly — cover shoulders and knees, especially at religious sites and in the medina. Casablanca is more cosmopolitan, but conservative dress is always appreciated.
◆ Remove shoes when entering homes, riads, or when indicated at mosques.
◆ Use your right hand for greetings, eating, and exchanging items. The left hand is considered unclean.
◆ Greetings matter — handshakes are common. Between close contacts, expect cheek kisses (usually two). Always greet before jumping into business.
◆ Ask before photographing people, especially in markets and rural areas.
Religion & Mosques
Islam in Daily Life
Morocco is a Muslim-majority country (99%+). The call to prayer (adhan) sounds five times daily — this is normal and part of the culture.

Most mosques are closed to non-Muslims. The major exception is the Hassan II Mosque in Casablanca, which offers guided tours and is on our itinerary.

Ramadan note: Ramadan 2026 ends around March 18. You may still see some observances early in the trip. Be respectful of those fasting — avoid eating/drinking conspicuously in public during daylight hours in the first day or two.
Food & Drink
What to Eat (and Not to Drink)

DO NOT drink tap water. Stick to bottled water. Use bottled water for brushing teeth too. Avoid ice in drinks unless at a high-end establishment.

Must-Try Dishes:
◆ Tagine — slow-cooked stew in a cone-shaped pot (lamb, chicken, or vegetable)
◆ Couscous — traditionally served on Fridays (sacred day)
◆ Tanjia — Marrakech specialty, slow-cooked meat
◆ Mint Tea — "Moroccan whiskey" — a social ritual, always accept when offered
◆ Pastilla — sweet and savory pastry with pigeon or chicken
Safety & Health
Stay Smart
Travel Advisory: Level 2 — Exercise Increased Caution (standard for most countries).

◆ Watch for petty theft in crowded areas (medinas, markets, transport hubs). Keep valuables in front pockets or a crossbody bag.
◆ Be firm with touts and hustlers — a polite but firm "La, shukran" (No, thank you) works.
◆ No mandatory vaccinations, but CDC recommends being up-to-date on MMR, Tdap, Hep A, and Typhoid.
◆ Emergency: Dial 19 (police) or 112 from a cell phone.
◆ Avoid walking alone at night in unfamiliar areas, especially in older medina neighborhoods.
Getting Around
Transportation
Rideshare Apps (recommended):
◆ InDrive — most popular, negotiate your price
◆ Careem — Uber-like, reliable in cities
◆ WinkTaxi — alternative option
Petits Taxis:
Small, color-coded taxis for getting around cities. Red in Casablanca, beige in Marrakech. Insist on the meter or agree on price before getting in. Short rides typically cost 10–20 MAD.
Business & Social Norms
For GIM Meetings
◆ Relationships first. Moroccans value personal connections before getting down to business. Expect small talk, tea, and hospitality before meetings begin.
◆ Meetings may start late. Punctuality expectations are more relaxed than in the U.S.
◆ Business casual / suit for plenary meetings (check Google Sheet for daily dress code).
◆ Sensitive topics to avoid: criticism of the King or monarchy, Western Sahara politics, and comparing Morocco unfavorably to other countries.
Quick Reference
Electricity
Type C & E plugs (same as France). 220V. Bring a European adapter — one plug works for Morocco AND Paris.
Time Zone
Morocco: UTC+1 (6 hrs ahead of Chicago). France: UTC+1 (same as Morocco in March).
Haggling
Expected in souks and markets. Start at 50% of asking price and negotiate. Fixed prices in modern stores and malls.
